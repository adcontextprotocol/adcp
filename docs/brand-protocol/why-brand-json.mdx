---
title: Why brand.json?
description: Why brands should publish machine-readable identity data for AI agents — and what happens when they don't.
---

AI agents are already generating content that references your brand. Ad creative tools, chatbots, search engines, and campaign planners all need your logo, colors, and tone of voice. Without a source of truth, they guess. Sometimes they guess wrong.

`brand.json` is a small file you host on your domain that tells AI agents exactly how to represent your brand. Think of it as your brand guidelines, formatted for machines.

## The problem: AI is already using your brand

Every day, AI systems make decisions about how your brand looks and sounds. They pick logo variants, choose colors for ad creatives, and write copy in what they think is your voice. They pull this information from wherever they can find it — cached web pages, third-party databases, outdated press kits.

You have no control over what they find or how they interpret it.

## What brand.json actually is

Your logo is on your website right now. Your brand colors are in your CSS. Your tagline is in your meta tags. All of this is already public. `brand.json` does not expose anything new — it structures what is already out there so AI agents get it right instead of getting it close.

It is a single file hosted at `yourdomain.com/.well-known/brand.json` that declares:

- **Who you are** — your name, logo, and brand hierarchy
- **How you look** — colors, fonts, visual style
- **How you sound** — tone of voice, messaging guidelines
- **What not to do** — restrictions on how AI can use your brand

## Tell AI what not to do

This is the feature brand managers care about most. The `restrictions` field lets you set explicit rules that generative AI systems must follow:

```
"Never place text over the product"
"Do not use black backgrounds"
"No stock photography of people on phones"
"No split-screen layouts"
```

Without these guardrails, every AI tool makes its own judgment. With them, your brand standards travel with your brand data.

## Two tiers: public and authorized

Not everything belongs in a public file. `brand.json` handles this with two access levels:

| Level | What it includes | Who sees it |
|-------|-----------------|-------------|
| **Public** | Name, logo, colors, tagline, basic tone | Any AI agent |
| **Authorized** | High-res assets, voice synthesis, detailed guidelines | Partners you approve |

The public tier contains what is already on your website. The authorized tier is for the assets and guidelines you share only with agencies and partners. You decide who gets access by linking accounts — no data leaks to agents you have not approved.

## Represent your actual brand portfolio

If your company owns multiple brands, `brand.json` represents that structure. A house (your corporate entity) contains brands, and each brand owns its digital properties.

Pinnacle Media Group might have three brands — Pinnacle News, Pinnacle Sports, and StreamMax — each with their own identity, colors, and tone. One `brand.json` file on the house domain captures the whole portfolio. Individual brand domains simply point back to the house.

## Start simple

The smallest useful `brand.json` is just a name and a logo:

```json
{
  "$schema": "https://adcontextprotocol.org/schemas/v3/brand.json",
  "version": "1.0",
  "house": { "domain": "novabrands.com", "name": "Nova Brands" },
  "brands": [{
    "id": "nova",
    "names": [{"en": "Nova Brands"}],
    "logos": [{"url": "https://novabrands.com/logo.svg"}]
  }]
}
```

Add colors when you are ready. Add tone guidelines later. Add visual restrictions when you see AI getting it wrong. The file grows with your needs.

## The competitive reality

Brands that publish `brand.json` get represented accurately by AI agents across the advertising ecosystem — correct logos, correct colors, correct voice, correct restrictions. Brands that do not publish one get whatever the AI decides based on whatever data it can scrape.

This gap will widen. As more campaign planning, creative production, and media buying moves to agentic workflows, the brands with machine-readable identity data will be the ones represented faithfully. The rest will spend time correcting mistakes that did not need to happen.

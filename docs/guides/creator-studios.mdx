You get 100,000 subscribers and YouTube sends you a silver play button. You film yourself unboxing it. A million subscribers and you get a gold one. You film that too. Ten million and you get diamond. You film that, and 50 million people watch you film it.

Somewhere along the way, the power dynamic flips. You start as YouTube's product. You end as YouTube's customer. Or competitor.

This is the creator economy progression. And it's why ad tech needs a new model.

## The convergence

Traditional ad tech assumes clean separations. Publishers publish. Advertisers advertise. Talent performs. Data companies provide data. Four different companies, four different business models, four different ad tech stacks.

A creator collapses all four into one person.

<img src="/docs/images/guides/creator-economy/convergence.png" alt="A creator stands at the center of four overlapping colored spotlights — publisher, advertiser, talent, data company — all converging into one person in their studio" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '12px', marginBottom: '2rem' }} />

MrBeast is the **publisher** — Beast Games runs on YouTube and Prime Video, producing episodes on a weekly schedule with 300M+ subscribers watching. He is the **advertiser** — Feastables and Beast Burger are DTC brands that buy media on the same platforms where his content runs. He is the **talent** — his face IS the creative; his likeness has commercial value independent of any specific show. And he is the **data company** — 300 million subscribers constitute one of the largest opted-in audience graphs in media.

This isn't unique to MrBeast. The Stokes Twins, Emma Chamberlain, Dude Perfect, Linden Lane Films — the pattern repeats at every scale. Content, commerce, and creative converge in a single entity. The question is how ad tech keeps up.

## Three stages of creator growth

Creators don't start as media companies. They grow into them. The journey follows a predictable pattern, and at each stage the advertising model — who sells, who buys, who owns the audience — changes fundamentally.

<img src="/docs/images/guides/creator-economy/three-stages.png" alt="Three connected panels showing a creator's journey: filming alone in a bedroom, producing in a professional studio, commanding a media empire from a glass office" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '12px', marginBottom: '2rem' }} />

### Stage 1: Distribution

You create content and put it on other people's platforms. YouTube, TikTok, Spotify. You're a guest in their house. They own the ad sales, the audience relationship, the data. You get a revenue share.

At this stage, the **platform** runs the AdCP sales agent. The creator's content appears as show metadata — a `talent` entry on a show object, referenced by YouTube's products. The creator controls one thing: their [brand.json](/docs/brand-protocol/brand-json), which establishes their identity and — if they choose — declares their likeness as licensable.

```json
{
  "talent": [
    {
      "role": "host",
      "name": "Alan Stokes",
      "brand_url": "https://stokeswins.example.com/.well-known/brand.json"
    },
    {
      "role": "host",
      "name": "Alex Stokes",
      "brand_url": "https://stokeswins.example.com/.well-known/brand.json"
    }
  ]
}
```

The `brand_url` is the seed. Even at Stage 1, it connects the creator's identity to the protocol. A buyer agent evaluating a YouTube product can follow that link to discover the talent behind it — and potentially license their likeness for creative, even if the platform controls the inventory.

**AdCP primitives**: `brand.json` (talent identity). That's it. The platform handles the rest.

### Stage 2: Syndication

The audience follows YOU, not the platform. Your fans find you on YouTube, then TikTok, then your podcast, then your CTV show. You start selling direct — brand deals, sponsorships, branded integrations. The platform still sells programmatic ads around your content, but you control the premium inventory.

At this stage, the **creator runs their own AdCP sales agent** alongside the platform's. They sell guaranteed sponsorships (flat rate, reserved episodes) while the platform sells scatter (non-guaranteed, CPM). The same show, same episodes, different products from different agents.

This is where AdCP's [show and episode model](/docs/media-buy/product-discovery/shows-and-episodes) becomes essential. The creator's agent declares shows with full cross-platform `distribution`, schedules episodes with temporal status (`scheduled`, `tentative`, `published`), and offers products at multiple commitment levels:

| What the creator sells | AdCP model |
|---|---|
| Season sponsorship | Guaranteed product, `show_ids`, `flat_rate` |
| Episode-specific deal | Guaranteed product with `episodes` array |
| Branded integration | `product_placement` channel with placements |
| Audience data | First-party signals via `get_signals` |
| Talent licensing | Rights via `get_rights` / `acquire_rights` |

| What the platform still sells | AdCP model |
|---|---|
| Pre-roll / mid-roll | Non-guaranteed product, CPM auction |
| Programmatic display | Standard ad products |

The creator's `distribution` array is what ties these together — a buyer agent can see that the same show exists on YouTube (where the platform sells scatter) and on the creator's own CTV app (where the creator sells direct).

```json
{
  "distribution": [
    {
      "publisher_domain": "youtube.com",
      "identifiers": [{ "type": "youtube_channel_id", "value": "UCX6OQ3DkcsbYNE6H8uQQuVA" }]
    },
    {
      "publisher_domain": "amazon.com",
      "identifiers": [{ "type": "amazon_title_id", "value": "B0DFBT5GBP" }]
    },
    {
      "publisher_domain": "beastgames.example.com",
      "identifiers": [{ "type": "domain", "value": "beastgames.example.com" }]
    }
  ]
}
```

**AdCP primitives**: Shows, episodes, products, signals, rights — the full protocol. The creator is simultaneously a seller (of their own inventory) and a buyer (of ads on other platforms for their DTC brand).

### Stage 3: Owned channel

You ARE the platform. Notre Dame doesn't need a conference. MrBeast doesn't need YouTube — YouTube needs MrBeast. You own the distribution, the audience data, the ad sales, the commerce. Your sales agent is the only one.

At this stage, the creator uses AdCP in both directions:

- **As a seller**: Full sales agent exposing shows, episodes, products, signals, and rights to any buyer agent
- **As a buyer**: Buyer agent purchasing media on YouTube, TikTok, and other platforms to promote their DTC brands
- **As a brand**: `brand.json` for product lines (Feastables, Beast Burger) with product catalogs and conversion events
- **As a signal provider**: Monetizing first-party fan community data as a standalone product

The protocol doesn't care whether you're a media conglomerate or a person with a camera. The primitives are the same. That's the point.

## How content becomes products

A single episode of creator content generates an entire product portfolio. The full episode airs on CTV. A highlight reel goes to YouTube Shorts. A behind-the-scenes clip goes to the secondary channel. A trailer for next week drops on TikTok. And the creator's likeness is licensable for AI-generated creative entirely separate from the show.

<img src="/docs/images/guides/creator-economy/episode-products.png" alt="A director's chair at center stage with five glowing paths radiating outward to different screens — TV, phone, monitor, tablet, and a holographic likeness — one episode becoming five products" style={{ width: '100%', borderRadius: '12px', marginBottom: '2rem' }} />

AdCP models this with three connected concepts:

- **`derivative_of`** — Highlights and clips reference their source episode, inheriting brand safety context
- **`related_shows`** — Companion content (behind-the-scenes, after-shows) links to the main show
- **Rights licensing** — The talent's likeness is a separate product, independent of any specific content

### Season sponsorship (upfront reservation)

The flagship deal. A brand locks in an entire season before it airs — guaranteed placement across every episode, with the studio's creative team handling integration. This is the creator equivalent of a television upfront buy.

```json
{
  "shows": [
    {
      "show_id": "beast_games_s2",
      "name": "Beast Games Season 2",
      "description": "Competition reality: 1,000 contestants, elimination challenges, $10M prize",
      "genre": ["IAB1", "IAB1-6"],
      "genre_taxonomy": "iab_content_3.0",
      "content_rating": { "system": "tv_parental", "rating": "TV-PG" },
      "cadence": "weekly",
      "season": "2",
      "status": "upcoming",
      "production_quality": "professional",
      "talent": [
        {
          "role": "host",
          "name": "Jimmy Donaldson",
          "brand_url": "https://mrbeast.example.com/.well-known/brand.json"
        }
      ],
      "distribution": [
        {
          "publisher_domain": "youtube.com",
          "identifiers": [{ "type": "youtube_channel_id", "value": "UCX6OQ3DkcsbYNE6H8uQQuVA" }]
        },
        {
          "publisher_domain": "amazon.com",
          "identifiers": [{ "type": "amazon_title_id", "value": "B0DFBT5GBP" }]
        }
      ]
    }
  ],
  "products": [
    {
      "product_id": "beast_games_s2_title_sponsor",
      "name": "Beast Games S2 — Title Sponsorship",
      "description": "Season-long title sponsorship across all 12 episodes. Includes branded integration in each episode, host mention, and logo placement in intro/outro.",
      "show_ids": ["beast_games_s2"],
      "channels": ["product_placement"],
      "publisher_properties": [
        { "publisher_domain": "youtube.com", "selection_type": "all" },
        { "publisher_domain": "amazon.com", "selection_type": "all" }
      ],
      "placements": [
        { "placement_id": "title_card", "name": "Title card (5s)" },
        { "placement_id": "integration", "name": "In-episode branded integration (60-90s)" },
        { "placement_id": "outro", "name": "Outro sponsor card (10s)" }
      ],
      "delivery_type": "guaranteed",
      "exclusivity": "category",
      "pricing_options": [
        {
          "pricing_option_id": "season_flat",
          "pricing_model": "flat_rate",
          "fixed_price": 5000000,
          "currency": "USD"
        }
      ],
      "delivery_measurement": {
        "provider": "YouTube Analytics + Amazon Prime Video Ads",
        "notes": "Combined viewership across YouTube and Prime Video. Episode-level delivery reports within 48 hours of publish."
      }
    }
  ]
}
```

- **`status: "upcoming"`** — the season hasn't started yet. The buyer is reserving inventory before production.
- **`exclusivity: "category"`** — only one brand per product category can hold the title sponsorship.
- **Cross-platform distribution** — the same show, same sponsorship, reaches audiences on YouTube and Prime Video.

### Episode-specific sponsorship

Not every brand wants a full season. Episode-specific products let buyers pick individual episodes — a premiere, a finale, or episodes with specific guests.

```json
{
  "product_id": "beast_games_s2_premiere",
  "name": "Beast Games S2 — Premiere Episode Sponsorship",
  "show_ids": ["beast_games_s2"],
  "channels": ["product_placement"],
  "episodes": [
    {
      "episode_id": "s2e01_the_drop",
      "name": "The Drop",
      "scheduled_at": "2026-09-15T20:00:00Z",
      "status": "scheduled",
      "duration_seconds": 5400,
      "ad_inventory": {
        "expected_breaks": 3,
        "total_ad_seconds": 270,
        "max_ad_duration_seconds": 90,
        "unplanned_breaks": false,
        "supported_formats": ["video"]
      },
      "guest_talent": [
        { "role": "guest", "name": "Gordon Ramsay" }
      ]
    }
  ],
  "placements": [
    { "placement_id": "integration", "name": "In-episode integration (60-90s)" },
    { "placement_id": "mid_roll", "name": "Mid-roll (30s)" }
  ],
  "delivery_type": "guaranteed",
  "pricing_options": [
    {
      "pricing_option_id": "premiere_flat",
      "pricing_model": "flat_rate",
      "fixed_price": 750000,
      "currency": "USD"
    }

```

The premiere has confirmed details — date, duration, guest talent. Later episodes might be `tentative`, with `valid_until` dates telling buyer agents when to re-check.

### Run-of-show (scatter)

For brands that want reach without committing to specific episodes — "your ad runs across whatever episodes air during your flight dates."

```json
{
  "product_id": "beast_games_s2_scatter",
  "name": "Beast Games S2 — Run of Show",
  "show_ids": ["beast_games_s2"],
  "channels": ["influencer"],
  "publisher_properties": [
    { "publisher_domain": "youtube.com", "selection_type": "all" }
  ],
  "placements": [
    { "placement_id": "pre_roll", "name": "Pre-roll (15s)" },
    { "placement_id": "mid_roll", "name": "Mid-roll (30s)" }
  ],
  "delivery_type": "non_guaranteed",
  "pricing_options": [
    {
      "pricing_option_id": "ros_cpm",
      "pricing_model": "cpm",
      "floor_price": 35.00,
      "price_guidance": { "p50": 45.00, "p75": 55.00 },
      "currency": "USD",
      "min_spend_per_package": 50000
    }
  ]
}
```

Non-guaranteed delivery, CPM pricing, lower commitment. The creator equivalent of scatter TV.

### The creator AS the creative

Here's where it gets interesting. A brand doesn't just want to run ads around creator content — they want the creator IN their ads. The Stokes Twins appearing in a Feastables commercial. MrBeast's voice in an AI-generated audio ad. A creator's likeness on a billboard.

This isn't inventory. It's creative. And AdCP models it through [Brand Protocol rights licensing](/docs/brand-protocol/for-rights-holders), not Media Buy.

The creator's `brand.json` declares licensable rights:

```json
{
  "name": "Jimmy Donaldson",
  "url": "https://mrbeast.example.com",
  "type": "talent",
  "rights": {
    "agent_url": "https://rights.mrbeast.example.com",
    "right_types": ["talent"],
    "summary": "Licensable likeness and voice for AI-generated advertising creative. Managed by Night Media."
  }
}
```

A buyer agent calls `get_rights`, receives pricing and scope, and calls `acquire_rights` to get generation credentials — scoped keys that authorize specific AI providers to generate content featuring the licensed talent. The creator's agency maintains approval rules, content restrictions, and geographic limits.

<Tip>
A brand might sponsor Beast Games (media buy through the studio's sales agent) AND license Jimmy's likeness for AI-generated display ads (rights acquisition through Brand Protocol). Two revenue streams, one sales agent, one brief.
</Tip>

## The audience is the asset

At Stage 1, YouTube owns the audience data. At Stage 3, the creator does. The progression from "YouTube knows who watches my videos" to "I know who my fans are, where they live, what they buy, and which platforms they use" is the real power shift.

Creator studios sit on first-party audience data that most publishers would envy. When fans opt into a community — subscribing, joining a membership tier, buying merch, downloading an app — they provide verified data. Not modeled lookalikes. Not probabilistic segments. Real people with real demographics.

AdCP's [Signals protocol](/docs/signals/overview) lets studios expose this data to buyer agents:

```json
{

    {
      "signal_id": "beast_verified_fans_18_34",
      "name": "Beast Games Verified Fans — 18-34",
      "description": "Opted-in fan community members aged 18-34 with verified demographics. Cross-platform engagement across YouTube, Prime Video, and Beast mobile app.",
      "signal_type": "audience",
      "reach": {
        "estimated_size": 12000000,
        "unit": "individuals"
      },
      "dimensions": [
        { "dimension": "age", "values": ["18-24", "25-34"] },
        { "dimension": "engagement", "values": ["high"] },
        { "dimension": "platform", "values": ["youtube", "prime_video", "mobile_app"] }
      ],
      "data_source": {
        "type": "first_party",
        "collection_method": "opt_in",
        "refresh_frequency": "daily"
      },
      "activation": {
        "pricing_model": "cpm",
        "price": 8.50,
        "currency": "USD"
      }
    },
    {
      "signal_id": "beast_purchasers",
      "name": "Feastables Purchasers",
      "description": "Fans who have purchased Feastables products. Deterministic purchase data from e-commerce and retail partners.",
      "signal_type": "audience",
      "reach": {
        "estimated_size": 3500000,
        "unit": "individuals"
      },
      "dimensions": [
        { "dimension": "purchase_recency", "values": ["30d", "90d", "365d"] },
        { "dimension": "purchase_channel", "values": ["ecommerce", "retail"] }

```

This is the creator equivalent of a retailer's first-party shopper data — except instead of purchase history at a store, it's engagement history with content. And because creators build audiences across platforms, the graph spans devices and surfaces in a way single-platform data cannot.

The Feastables purchaser segment is particularly powerful. This is a creator who has closed the loop between content and commerce — they know not just who watches, but who buys. That's deterministic purchase data at scale, available as a signal for any buyer agent.

## Selling content before it exists

Creator studios produce content on a schedule. A weekly YouTube series has a known cadence. A seasonal CTV show has a planned episode count. A live event has a date. This temporal structure is what makes creator inventory reservable.

### The reservation spectrum

| Buying mode | AdCP model | Commitment |
|---|---|---|
| **Upfront** (full season) | Guaranteed, `show_ids` only, `flat_rate` | Highest — locked in before production |
| **Episode reservation** | Guaranteed, specific `episodes` | High — specific dates and content |
| **Scatter** | Non-guaranteed, `show_ids` only, CPM | Medium — any episode during flight |
| **Back-catalog** | Non-guaranteed, `status: "published"` | Lowest — already-aired content |

### Tentative inventory

Not all content is confirmed in advance. A creator might plan 12 episodes but only have the first 4 in production:

```json
{
  "episode_id": "s2e08",
  "name": "TBD — Production in progress",
  "scheduled_at": "2026-11-03T20:00:00Z",
  "status": "tentative",
  "valid_until": "2026-10-15T00:00:00Z"
}
```

A `tentative` episode with a `valid_until` date tells the buyer agent: this might happen, but check back before committing budget. This is how studios sell against their production pipeline without overpromising.

## Best practices

### For creator studios (sellers)

1. **Model shows with full distribution** — include all platforms in `distribution` so buyer agents can evaluate cross-platform reach
2. **Declare talent with brand.json links** — connect talent to their identity via `brand_url` so buyers can evaluate brand safety and discover licensing in one step
3. **Offer multiple buying modes** — season sponsorships (guaranteed, flat rate), episode-specific (guaranteed, per-episode), scatter (non-guaranteed, CPM), and highlights for the same show
4. **Use episode status honestly** — mark episodes `tentative` when production isn't confirmed. Include `valid_until`. Overselling tentative inventory damages trust
5. **Expose audience data via signals** — your first-party fan community data is a product. Surface it through `get_signals` with clear data source declarations
6. **Connect derivative content** — link highlights, clips, and BTS to source episodes via `derivative_of`
7. **Set channels explicitly** — `product_placement` for branded integrations, `influencer` for standard ad placements

### For brands (buyers)

1. **Start with the brief** — describe what you need in natural language. The studio's agent matches your brief to shows, episodes, and audience segments
2. **Validate audience with signals** — query `get_signals` for verified first-party data before committing budget
3. **Mix buying modes** — guaranteed sponsorship anchors brand presence; scatter fills gaps; highlights extend the tail
4. **Check talent availability early** — if you want the creator IN your ads, query `get_rights` during planning. Exclusivity windows may affect timing
5. **Use episode-level delivery data** — `by_episode` breakdowns reveal which content drives engagement
6. **Evaluate cross-platform distribution** — the same show on YouTube, Prime Video, and TikTok reaches different segments on each surface
